The electronic and digital age (20th century)

Computers (1940s-1970s)

The computers developed between the 1940s and 1970 marked the beginning of a major technological revolution, profoundly transforming society and working patterns. These machines, originally designed to perform complex calculations, have rapidly evolved into tools Versatile applications in various fields, from scientific research to administration.
The first electronic computer, ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer), was built in 1945 in the United States. This massive, room-filling machine uses vacuum tubes to perform calculations and can perform operations that took human beings hours to complete in a matter of seconds. However, these first computers remain expensive, cumbersome and accessible only to large institutions. The first electronic computer, ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer), is built in 1945 in the United States. This massive, full-room machine uses vacuum tubes to perform calculations and can perform operations that took humans hours to complete in seconds. However, these first computers remain expensive, cumbersome and accessible only to large institutions.
In the 1950s, the introduction of transistors replaced vacuum tubes, making computers more reliable, less energy-intensive and more compact. Machines like the IBM 701 mark the beginning of the computerization of businesses, especially for processing financial data and administrative tasks. This period also saw the emergence of the first programming languages such as Fortran and COBOL, which simplified interaction with computers.
The 1960s marked a new stage with the introduction of integrated circuits, which further miniaturized components and increased computing power. This led to the creation of more affordable machines, such as the PDP-8 from Digital Equipment Corporation, often considered the first mini-computer. At the same time, IT is becoming more democratic in areas such as education and research.
In the 1970s, microprocessors revolutionized computing by making it possible to make personal computers. Machines like the Altair 8800 are paving the way for home-friendly computing. It was also the time when operating systems, such as Unix, began to Standardize the use of computers. Between 1940 and 1970, the computers are therefore moving from gigantic tools for the scientific and industrial elites to smaller, more powerful machines suitable for a variety of uses. This period lays the foundation for modern computing and the digital revolution that will follow.

Word processing

Word processing is a technology that has transformed the way people write, edit and produce written documents, gradually replacing traditional typewriters. Introduced in the 1960s it combines the capabilities of computers with specially designed writing software. Early word processing systems, such as the MT/ST (Magnetic Tape/Selectric Typewriter) from IBM, used an electric typewriter coupled with magnetic tapes to record and reuse text
With the rise of personal computers in the 1980s, word processing became more accessible and interactive. Software such as WordStar launched in 1979 or Microsoft Word introduced in 1983 offers advanced features compared to manual or mechanical writing. These programmes not only enable Write, but also easily edit text, insert images, change fonts and apply a complex layout.
The main advantage of word processing is its flexibility and its ease of use. It eliminates the need to restart an entire document in case of error, thanks to the quick delete and correction functions. Users can save their files, print them, or share them digitally, revolutionizing processes working in offices, schools and homes.
Over time, word processing has evolved to incorporate additional tools such as spelling and grammar, speech recognition, and even suggestions for style improvements. These innovations have significantly increased the productivity and quality of the output. With the advent of cloud computing, applications like Google Docs now allow for real-time collaboration between multiple users, marking a new milestone in the history of word processing. Word processing has become a must-have tool in all areas. Not only has it simplified document creation, but it has also paved the way for more efficient management of written information, making it a key technology in the digital revolution.

E-books (e-books)

Electronic books, or e-books, are a revolution in the world of reading and publishing. The technology, which was first developed in the 1970s with the Project Gutenberg initiative to digitize literary works free of copyright, enables reading books in digital form on various devices such as computers, tablets, smartphones and e-readers.
A e-book is a digital file containing text and sometimes images, accessible through specific formats such as EPUB, PDF or MOBI. Its main feature is its portability: a single reader can hold thousands of books, making it ideal for avid readers or those who travel frequently. The introduction of the first commercial e-readers, such as Amazon in 2007, democratized the e-books offering instant access to vast digital libraries via online platforms.
There are several advantages to using e-books. They allow you to adjust the size and style of the font and provide a reading that is tailored to your needs, especially for visually impaired people. In addition, features such as annotations, bookmarks, quick search and integrated translation enhance the reading experience. They are also ecological, reducing the consumption of paper and the costs associated with producing and transporting physical books. However, the e-books also pose challenges. The issues of digital rights (DRM) and ownership are controversial, as e-book buyers often only have a license to use. Some readers also regret the loss of tactile and sensory experience of the paper books.
Despite these limitations, e-books have become an essential complement to traditional formats. They provide increased accessibility to reading, especially in areas where physical books are scarce or expensive. In the educational setting, they allow for manuals and interactive resources to be grouped into a single device, transforming learning. By combining practicality, innovation and ecology, the e-books have redefined our relationship to reading while coexisting with the paper books that remain rooted in the cultural and sentimental traditions of the readers.

Internet (1990)

Internet, launched commercially in the 1990s, transformed the world into an interconnected network, revolutionizing communication, commerce, and access to information. Its origins date back to the 1960s with ARPANET, a project of the US Department of Defense designed to share data between computers. However, it was in 1990 that the British researcher Tim Berners-Lee Invents the World Wide Web, a user-friendly interface for easy navigation on the Internet via hypertext pages.
The arrival of Internet for the general public coincides with major advances such as the creation of web browsers, such as Mosaic in 1993 and Netscape in 1994, which simplify access to the network. This period also marks the emergence of Internet providers that allow millions of households to connect from their personal computers. From there, the Internet becomes a global platform for exchanging emails, search for information and interact via forums or websites.
One of the greatest assets of the Internet is its role in democratizing information. Users can instantly access educational, cultural and scientific content, transforming the way knowledge is produced and shared. The e-commerce industry, with pioneers such as Amazon and eBay, is growing rapidly, changing consumer habits. Internet also redefined social communication. The emergence of instant messengers and, later, the social networks in the 2000 years, brought individuals closer together despite distances, while creating new challenges in terms of confidentiality and cybersecurity.
Despite its immense potential, the Internet also raises challenges such as unequal access, the spread of false information and digital dependency. These issues continue to feed the debates on ethical and responsible use of the network. Since the 1990s, Internet has become a key pillar of daily and professional life. It redefined human relations, accelerated innovation and laid the foundations for an interconnected world where geographical boundaries are being replaced by a global economy and a global culture.
