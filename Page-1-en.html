The Antiquity and the Origins of Writing (around 3000 BC)

Cuneiform Writing

The cuneiform script, which appeared in Mesopotamia around 3300 BC, is considered to be one of the earliest systems of writing history. Developed by the Sumerians, it was originally used for administrative purposes and accountants, particularly to record trade flows and manage agricultural commodity stocks. The term "cuneiform" comes from the Latin cuneus, meaning "corner", in reference to the shape of the characters in nails or corners, obtained by pressing a cut calama from a reed rod onto wet clay tablets. Once engraved, these tablets were dried sun or baked to ensure sustainability.
Originally, the cuneiform script was pictographic: each symbol represented an object or idea. For example, a head drawing de b≈ìuf meant "beef". Over time, these designs have been simplified and standardized, evolving into a system of abstract and phonetic signs. This evolution has allowed to transcribe not only proper names and complex ideas, but also various languages such as Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian and Assyrian, which has encouraged its adoption in a vast region of the Middle East.
Beyond the simple economic management, cuneiform writing has been used for religious texts, codes of laws (such as the famous Code of Hammurabi), literary narratives (such as The Epic of Gilgamesh), and historical documents. It allowed the creation of the first libraries, such as that of Assurbanipal Nineveh, where thousands of tablets were found.This system lasted for more than 3,000 years, before gradually disappearing with the advent of simpler alphabets such as those of the Phoenicians.
The cuneiform script represents a major turning point in human history, marking the passage from prehistory to history and paving the way for conservation and the lasting transmission of knowledge through the ages. Today, its decipherment in the 19th century has allowed a better understanding of ancient civilizations and their contribution to our cultural heritage

Papyrus

Papyrus is one of the earliest known writing materials, used mainly by ancient Egyptian civilizations from 3000 BC. Made from the plant of the same name, which grew in abundance along the Nile, it represented a major advance compared to the clay tablets used in Mesopotamia. The manufacture of papyrus consisted to cut the stems of the plant into thin strips, which were arranged in crossed layers, hammered out and then glued together with natural sap. After drying, a sheet was obtained flexible, lightweight and easy to carry.
Papyrus was used to write with brushes or calames dipped in soot-based ink or natural pigments. It was mainly used to record religious, administrative and literary, but also letters and legal contracts. The papyrus sheets could be assembled into rolls, making it easier to store and consult documents. These scrolls, called volumes, were often stored in libraries, such as the famous library of Alexandria.
The use of papyrus spread rapidly throughout the Mediterranean basin thanks to Egyptian trade. However, its fragility in the face of humidity and its relatively high cost limited its use to important documents. From the 3rd century AD, it was gradually replaced by parchment, made from animal skins, which was more durable and better suited to humid climates.
Despite this, the papyrus remains a symbol of early writing and knowledge transmission. The fragments found today, thanks to the arid conditions of Egypt, offer us valuable testimonies On the culture, religion and daily life of antiquity. It is an essential milestone in the history of writing media, linking early civilizations to later technological advances.

Stylus and Wax Tablets

Stylus and wax tablets were widely used writing tools in antiquity, especially by the Greeks and Romans. The wax tablet consisted of a wooden frame filled with a thin wax layer, forming a reusable surface for writing. The stylus, or stylet, was a sharp instrument made of metal, bone or wood, used to engrave letters or numbers in wax. The other end of the stylus, flattened, allowed to erase the inscriptions by smoothing the wax, making this practical support for temporary writings.
These tablets were used for a variety of tasks, from taking notes and writing administrative documents to teaching. Students, for example, used them to practice writing and mathematics. The tablets could be connected together to form diptychs or triptychs, thus increasing the space available for writing, making them ideal for longer texts such as contracts or letters.
The main advantage of wax tablets was their reusability, in contrast to more expensive media such as papyrus or parchment. They were particularly popular in environments where writing was required to be frequently changed or corrected. However, their durability was limited: the wax could melt under the effect of heat or deteriorate over time.
These tools were widely used throughout the Roman Empire and remained in use for several centuries, until the advent of paper. Their simplicity and economic character have made them essential supports for everyday life, including for rapid communication and learning tasks. Wax stylus and tablets are now a fascinating testimony to the creativity of ancient civilizations in meeting their practical needs for writing.
