Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) (20th - 21st century)

Augmented Reality (1990s)

Augmented reality (AR), developed in the 1990s, is a technology that superimposes digital elements on our real-time environment. Unlike virtual reality, which immerses the user in an entirely virtual world, AR enriches the physical world by adding visual and audio information or interactive, visible through devices such as smart glasses, smartphones or tablets.
The concept of augmented reality took shape in the 1960s with the first visualization systems, but it was in the 1990s that technology became more concrete. In 1992, researcher Tom Caudell introduced the term "augmented reality" to describe a tool used by Boeing to assist technicians in aircraft assembly. Soon after, researchers design systems combining cameras, sensors and displays to insert virtual images into the user‚Äôs field of view.
AR first finds applications in professional fields. It is used for training surgeons, industrial maintenance and army, where it provides critical information directly to the field of vision of soldiers. In architecture and design, AR facilitates the visualization of projects by integrating 3D models into real environments.
In the 2000 years, the rise of smartphones and integrated cameras democratizes AR. At the same time, innovations like HoloLens from Microsoft or Magic Leap helmets push AR‚Äôs limits in the education, health and commerce sectors.
Augmented reality is also transforming the user experience in areas such as marketing, retail or tourism, offering immersive and interactive solutions. It allows, for example, to test furniture in the living room before buying or to view lost historic monuments. Although promising, AR raises technological challenges such as the computing power required, as well as ethical issues related to privacy and dependency. Nevertheless, it remains a rapidly expanding technology with immense potential to improve the interaction between the physical and digital worlds.

Virtual reality (1980s - present)

Virtual reality (VR) is an immersive technology that immerses the user in a simulated environment of 3D, often interactive. This experience is made possible by helmets with displays, motion sensors and sometimes manual controllers, which allow you to navigate and interact in a virtual world. Although its first The foundations are laid as early as 1960, VR begins to take concrete form in the 1980s thanks to significant technological advances.
The term "virtual reality" is popularized by Jaron Lanier, founder of VPL Research, which develops VR helmets and data gloves for touch interaction with digital environments. These systems, although cutting edge, are expensive and limited by the computing power of the time, which limits their adoption to specialized sectors such as research, medicine or military training.
In the 1990s, VR attracted the attention of the general public through experiments such as arcade simulators or immersive devices presented by companies like SEGA and Nintendo. However, these early attempts are hampered by cost issues, low resolution and limited effectiveness, preventing mass adoption. The beginning of 2010 marked a new era in VR with the introduction of more powerful and affordable helmets such as the Oculus Rift, followed by competing products like the HTC Vive, the PlayStation VR or the Meta Quest. These devices use high definition displays, precise sensors and sophisticated algorithms to provide immersive experience without previous. The VR then extends to many areas, including video games, vocational training, medicine, architecture and even virtual tourism.
Today, VR continues to evolve with the integration of technologies such as eye tracking, mixed reality (which combines VR and augmented reality), and haptic accessories that enhance the sensory experience. It also opens up new perspectives in areas such as education by allowing realistic simulations, or wellness, by providing a soothing environment. Despite its promises, VR still has challenges to overcome, especially in terms of cost, accessibility and comfort (cybersickness). However, it remains a key technology in digital transformation, with unlimited potential to reinvent our interactions with the virtual world.

Mixed Reality (21st century)

The mixed reality (MR) that emerged in the 21st century combines the characteristics of virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR), allowing interactive digital objects to be integrated into a real-world environment while providing deeper immersion than AR alone. It allows the user to interact simultaneously with physical and virtual elements, while maintaining an awareness of their physical environment. This concept was popularized by companies like Microsoft with its HoloLens headset, which is one of the first devices to offer a mixed reality experience.
Unlike AR, which overlays digital information on the real world, AR allows for a more complex interaction between the real and virtual worlds. For example, virtual objects can be placed in a real space and respond to user actions more realistically, as if the virtual object was actually there. This type of interaction is rendered Advanced technologies such as object recognition, 3D mapping and motion detection.
The applications of mixed reality are wide and varied. In the field of medicine, it is used for surgical simulations, allowing professionals to practice in a virtual environment before performing real operations. In education, RM transforms learning by creating immersive experiences where students can interact with 3D models of objects or concepts, making complex materials more accessible. In the architecture sector, mixed reality allows architects and clients to visualize projects in 3D in a real space before they are built.
Mixed reality is also growing in the field of entertainment, with games and films that use interactive virtual elements in a real setting. In the business world, RM finds applications in training, remote collaboration and product design. By combining the benefits of AR and VR, mixed reality paves the way for More fluid and dynamic digital experiences that were not possible before. Although RM is still a developing technology, it already has the potential to profoundly transform industrial, educational and creative sectors. With the continuous improvement of interfaces and devices, the mixed reality could well become an essential tool in the evolution of our interaction with the digital world.
