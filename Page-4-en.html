The Enlightenment and industrialization (18th - 19th century)

Rotary press (19th century)

The rotary press, invented in the 19th century, marks a major milestone in the history of printing and the dissemination of information. Its principle is based on rotary cylinders that allow large quantities of text and images to be printed quickly on paper rolls. This invention, attributed to the German engineer Friedrich Koenig and perfected by Richard March Hoe in the USA, replaces the much slower platinum presses used since Gutenberg.
The press is booming at a time when print needs are exploding, particularly with the rise of the print media, increasing literacy and expanding newspapers. Thanks to its production speed, it allows to print thousands of copies in a few hours, making newspapers and books accessible to a much wider audience. In addition, production costs are falling, which is conducive to lower publication prices.
Improvements made throughout the 19th century, such as the use of continuous paper (rather than single sheets) and simultaneous double-sided printing, further enhance its efficiency. These advances allow the creation of large-circulation newspapers, such as daily newspapers popular, which become a key vector of information and education for the masses.
The press also influences other sectors, such as book publishing, advertising and industrial printing. It contributes to the standardization of publications and the development of visual communication through the progressive integration of illustrations and photographs. This technology has profoundly transformed society by democratizing access to information and accelerating the spread of ideas. By making a free and affordable press possible, the rotary press has played a central role in the emergence of modern democracies and industrialization of culture. Today, although it has evolved with digital technologies, its legacy remains at the heart of contemporary printing systems.

Typewriter

The typewriter, invented in the 19th century, transformed the world of writing and administration by automating the manual transcription of texts. Before his invention, writing was done exclusively by hand, which was long and impractical, especially for repetitive tasks such as Writing letters and documents. The first commercially viable typewriter model was designed in 1868 by Christopher Latham Sholes, an American journalist, in collaboration with Samuel Soule and Carlos Glidden. The patent for this machine was sold to the company Remington, which launched the first commercial typewriter in 1873.
The typewriter uses a keyboard of keys, each corresponding to a letter or symbol, which when pressed hit an ink ribbon to print characters on paper. This innovation allows typing to be much faster than by hand and produces more uniform documents. Mechanical typewriters quickly became popular in offices, revolutionizing administration, journalism and personal correspondence.
Typewriters have become indispensable tools in many sectors during the 20th century, including companies, news agencies and public administrations. Their influence is also felt in domestic life, especially through the appearance of portable and affordable from the 1920s. At the same time, specialized models such as the electric typewriter, which allowed typing to be faster and quieter, were born.
The invention of the typewriter also changed the role of secretaries and typists, whose typing skills became essential. However, starting in the 1980s, personal computers began to replace typewriters, offering additional features such as Automatic correction and digital text processing. Despite its gradual disappearance, the typewriter has left a lasting mark on culture, symbolizing an era when speed and productivity were directly related to mechanical writing. Education

Education, in its broadest sense, refers to the whole process by which an individual learns and develops throughout his or her life. It includes not only the acquisition of knowledge and practical skills, but also the learning of values, social norms and behaviours that allow for integration in society. Historically, education has evolved in response to the social, political and economic structures of each era. In ancient societies, education was often reserved for the elites and was by oral transmission or through handwritten manuscripts. The teaching was centred on religious and philosophical knowledge. However, with the invention of writing and then printing, access to knowledge gradually became more democratic, especially from the Renaissance.
Modern education took shape in the 19th century with the establishment of public school systems in many countries. The goal is to provide mass education, enabling all children to access basic knowledge. Education is becoming more structured, with school curricula, specialized subjects and a focus on science, language and mathematics. At this time, school became an essential tool for civic education and social mobility. In the 20th century, education diversified with the rise of new disciplines, the internationalization of educational exchanges, and the inclusion of all social strata. Educational reforms also aim to adapt education to technological, economic and cultural changes. Higher education is developing, and universities are becoming places of research and innovation.
Today, education continues to transform with the integration of digital technologies, the focus on lifelong learning and the equalization of educational opportunities in many parts of the world. Education plays a crucial role in personal development, social cohesion and economic progress, while being a pillar of democracy and social justice. The major challenge remains to make education accessible to all, regardless of their origin, social status or geography.
